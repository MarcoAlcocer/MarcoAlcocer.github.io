Data Projects

Mapping Criminal Organizations


Mapping Criminal Organizations in Mexico is an original effort to generate comprehensive information about the geography of criminal operations in Mexico. The project combines qualitative and automated methods, drawing from multiple sources, to produce a series of systematic datasets on the presence, structure, evolution, and relationships of criminal groups in Mexico. Data includes:

  • Signoret, Patrick; Marco Alcocer; Cecilia Farfan-Mendez; Fernanda Sobrino, 2021, "Mapping Criminal Organizations in Mexico: State Panel 2007-2015", https://doi.org/10.7910/DVN/N0KGCZ, Harvard Dataverse, V1.
  • Municipality-year data on presence 2000-2018, all municipalities, based on machine-aided data collection and classification.(Forthcoming)
  • Group histories tracking the origin, composition, and evolution of criminal groups.(Forthcoming)
  • Group dyad data on relationship status (allied, enemy, neutral) by period.(Forthcoming)

This project is supported by the Center for U.S.–Mexican Studies at UCSD’s School of Global Policy and Strategy, the Empirical Studies of Conflict Project, and the Data-Driven Social Science Initiative at Princeton Univeersity. It has also received funding from the Mamdouha S. Bobst Center for Peace and Justice at Princeton University.

Our online platform contains maps and other visuals and provides free access to the data, its codebooks, and methodology documents.

www.mexicocrimemaps.org (launch in October 2021)

Research

Dissertation

Crackdowns, Organized Crime Expansion, and Government Capture

My book-style dissertation investigates how the Mexican government’s crackdown of 2007 created incentives for organized crime to diversify their activities and expand their presence to territories where they had previously not operated. It then traces how criminal organizations have attempted, often successfully, to influence public policy by capturing local governments institutions, including public security agencies and local councils, in these new territories. To examine these questions my research relies on multi-method research, including quasi-experiments and qualitative methods.

Working Papers

"From Drug Cartels to Criminal Enterprises: Government Crackdowns and Organized Crime Diversification Strategies"

Abstract

In this paper I argue that government crackdowns against drug trafficking organizations (DTOs) can create incentives that push these organizations to diversify their activities. When lucrative new markets are found but cannot be fully exploited in the territories where DTOs are present, DTOs then face incentives to expand their presence to new territories where these lucrative new markets can be fully exploited. It then tests this theory in Mexico, where it first provides evidence that the Mexican government’s crackdown of 2007 led DTOs to diversify beyond drug trafficking. It then exploits the fact that one new lucrative market DTOs diversified to could only be done in specific places: oil theft from underground pipelines. Using a difference-in-differences design, I show that the crackdown caused DTOs to strategically expand to municipalities with oil pipelines. I then explore the implications of this expansion. I instrument DTO presence using the location of the pipelines to show that DTO expansion had major consequences for violence and other criminal activity, such as extortion, kidnapping, and drug dealing.
